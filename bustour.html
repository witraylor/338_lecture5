Walking Through the Celtic Tiger

Dublin's Boom, Bust, and Reinvention with Dr. Darren Kelly

Our first full day in Dublin began with orientation, followed by lunch, and then a bus tour in the afternoon. Luckily, our guide Darragh was up to the task of fighting jet lag and food comas to introduce us to Dublin. Instead of focusing on one neighborhood, we moved across several important sites connected to Irish politics, public memory, and national identity. Boarding the bus at CEA CAPA, we only traveled a short distance before reaching St. Stephen's Green.

Wolfe Tone Memorial and St. Stephen's Green

Our first stop was the Wolfe Tone Memorial at St. Stephen's Green. Theobald Wolfe Tone has one of the coolest names in Irish history, but more importantly, he was a central figure in the development of Irish republicanism. A lawyer, revolutionary, and founding leader of the Society of United Irishmen, Tone wanted to unite Protestants, Catholics, and Dissenters in the cause of Irish independence.

He first pushed for parliamentary reform and Catholic emancipation, but later turned toward armed rebellion with support from Revolutionary France. After a failed French-backed expedition during the 1798 Rebellion, he was captured and became a martyr figure in Irish nationalist memory. Behind his memorial we also saw Delaney's Irish Famine Memorial.

An added treat at this stop was the arrival of a group from The Wolfhound Experience, a tour accompanied by trained Irish Wolfhounds. The dogs were a hit with everyone nearby, and their presence added a very Irish, unexpected, and memorable moment to the tour.

Phoenix Park: Power, Nature, and Violence

Our next stop took us out to Phoenix Park. The park felt completely different from the city streets around St. Stephen's Green. It was open, green, and spacious, with animals, trees, and long roads that made it feel almost separate from Dublin. Phoenix Park is one of the largest enclosed public parks in a European capital, and it has served many purposes over time: royal hunting park, public recreation space, political setting, and national symbol.

Yes, they still do fox hunting.

While in Phoenix Park, we saw Áras an Uachtaráin, the official residence of the President of Ireland. Across the street was a small, unmarked memorial to the Phoenix Park Murders, a brutal political attack in 1882.

The General Post Office and the Easter Rising

After a quick stop at Collin's Barracks we headed to the General Post Office, usually called the GPO. Located on O'Connell Street, the GPO is one of the most important buildings in modern Irish history. During the Easter Rising of 1916, Irish rebels occupied the building and used it as their headquarters. It was outside the GPO that Patrick Pearse read the Proclamation of the Irish Republic, declaring Ireland's independence from British rule.

According to Darragh, almost everyone has a story of one of their family members being part of the Easter Rising — either as a rebel or a looter. The marks of the battle, including bullet holes and burn marks, can still be seen throughout the vicinity.

By the end of the tour, we were all tired, but definitely feeling more aware of the origins of some street names, surnames, and memorials in town.
