Walking Through the Celtic Tiger

Dublin's Boom, Bust, and Reinvention with Dr. Darren Kelly

Taking a guided tour through Dublin is always an incredible experience, but walking the city with a cultural geographer turned the afternoon into something much more meaningful than sightseeing. Our class toured the Dublin Docklands with Dr. Darren Kelly, and instead of simply pointing out buildings, he helped us “read” the city as evidence of Ireland's economic transformation. Standing in front of the Custom House and later looking across the glass skyline near the Samuel Beckett Bridge, we began to understand how Dublin moved from a place associated with limited opportunity and emigration to one of the world's major centers for technology, finance, and pharmaceuticals.

Dr. Kelly repeated an idea that stayed with me throughout the tour: “To understand Ireland, you need to understand its past.” That made our starting point at the Custom House feel intentional. Completed in 1791, the building once represented imperial trade and British administration. On our tour, however, it became more than a historic landmark. It was a starting point for thinking about how power, economics, geography, and history have shaped Ireland. As we walked toward the Docklands, we saw Dublin less as a collection of beautiful buildings and more as a map of change.

From Emigration to Education

One of the most interesting parts of the tour was learning that Ireland's economic rise did not begin with tech companies or glass office buildings. It began with education. In the 1980s, Ireland had many well-educated young people, but not enough university places or jobs for them. Many people had to leave Ireland to find work in Britain, the United States, or elsewhere.

Education is often presented as a direct path to opportunity. In Ireland's case, the talent was there before the economy was ready to use it.

Over time, the Irish government invested more heavily in higher education, colleges, and technical training. This helped create a skilled workforce in areas such as engineering, pharmaceuticals, banking, and computing. At the same time, Ireland used low corporate tax rates and other incentives to attract multinational companies.

The IFSC and the Celtic Tiger

When we reached the IFSC, the International Financial Services Centre, this shift became easier to see. Created in the late 1980s, the IFSC represented Ireland's move away from traditional manufacturing and toward finance, electronic services, and global business. Standing there, we could see how government policy, education, and geography came together to reshape the city.

This transformation helped fuel the "Celtic Tiger," Ireland's period of rapid economic growth from the 1990s into the mid-2000s. Money flowed into Dublin, and the city changed quickly. New offices, restaurants, shops, and apartment buildings appeared. Dr. Kelly showed us some of the first major apartment buildings connected to this new urban lifestyle, built for higher-paid workers who wanted to live near the expanding financial and tech districts. Apartment buildings are more than just evidence of economic change, they tell a story of evolvign lifestyles.

The Docklands: A New Skyline

The most memorable moment for me was near the quay and the Samuel Beckett Bridge, where we compared older images of the Docklands with the skyline in front of us. The contrast was dramatic. In the photos, Grand Canal Dock looked empty, industrial, and unfinished, with warehouses, mills, gasworks, and construction sites. In front of us were glass offices, expensive apartments, and the headquarters of major global companies. It felt like we were standing between two versions of Dublin at once.

We crossed the Samuel Beckett Bridge toward Google's Dublin campus, often called “Google Docks.” The bridge itself, shaped like a harp, felt symbolic: a national image placed in the middle of a global business district. Nearby places such as Boland's Mill, Windmill Lane, and the gasometer sites showed how working-class and industrial spaces had been transformed into offices, apartments, and cultural landmarks.

The Cost of Growth

The tour also made clear that economic success has not benefited everyone equally. The housing boom connected to the Celtic Tiger collapsed after the 2008 financial crisis. Ireland took on enormous banking debts, construction stalled, and unemployment rose. What stood out to me was how some public and distressed property assets were later sold to private investors and global firms. This helped me understand why Dublin's housing crisis is not just about supply and demand, but also about who owns the city and who gets to live in the spaces created by economic growth.

By the end of the tour, Dublin's Docklands felt like more than a modern business district. Before the tour, we probably would have seen the glass buildings, corporate offices, and new apartments mostly as signs of progress. After walking through the area with Dr. Kelly, we saw them as evidence of a more complicated story — one that includes education, emigration, foreign investment, opportunity, displacement, and housing pressure. The tour showed me that cities do not just change physically; they reveal who has power, who benefits from growth, and who is left struggling to keep up.

Our Guide

Darren Kelly, PhD holds a BA in English and Geography and a PhD in Cultural Geography from Dublin City University. He is a lecturer at Marino Institute of Education, Trinity College Dublin, where he developed and teaches on the Postgraduate Diploma in Further Education, and his scholarship focuses on using the city as both text and laboratory within international education. A Fulbright Scholar in Residence at Beloit College, Dr Kelly is widely published and recognized for his expertise in field-based learning, study abroad, intercultural intelligence, and the relationship between academic theory and experiential learning.
