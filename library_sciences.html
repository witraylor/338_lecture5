Front-End Accessibility in Library Digital Services

Speaker Session with Anna Leary, RCSI

Overview

Our session on front-end web development in library digital services was led by Anna Leary, Library Systems and Digital Services Coordinator at the Royal College of Surgeons in Ireland. This was a shorter and more technical session, but it connected directly to a major theme of our course: access. Instead of thinking about accessibility as only a web design requirement, we learned that accessibility shapes how people seek, discover, and use knowledge.

One of the main ideas that stood out was that libraries are built on equal access to information. Because of that, every design choice either supports or weakens that mission. A confusing button label, missing heading, or unclear error message might seem small, but it can determine whether someone successfully finds what they need or gives up entirely.

What Are Library Digital Services?

At first, our class guessed that library digital services might include catalog searches, digitized archives, chatbots, access to paywalled materials, printers, citation tools, and databases. Anna expanded that definition by describing library digital services as a complex ecosystem of connected platforms. These include discovery systems and catalogues, archives and repositories, databases and resources, booking systems, and library websites.

Accessibility Challenges

A major challenge is that libraries do not always control the systems they use. Many platforms rely on outside vendors rather than in-house development. This can make accessibility difficult because libraries may have limited control over the core design, limited front-end programming knowledge, and older interfaces that still need to serve many different users. Anna also pointed out that vendor updates can sometimes introduce new accessibility problems, called regressions.

The session made it clear that small choices can have a big impact. Ambiguous buttons, poor page structure, missing headings, and inaccessible error messages can all create barriers. These barriers do not always announce themselves. Instead, they show up as extra effort, confusion, loss of orientation, and a decline in user confidence.

Information Seeking and Accessibility

Anna connected accessibility to Information Seeking Behaviour theory. This helped move the conversation beyond simple compliance checklists. The point is not only to meet technical standards, but to design systems that match the real human experience of searching for information.

She mentioned several models, including Wilson's Information Seeking Behaviour model, Kuhlthau's Information Search Process, Bates' Berry Picking Model, and Bates' Integrated Model. These theories show that searching for information is rarely a straight path. Users explore, change direction, get confused, reformulate searches, and gradually build understanding. Accessible design supports that process instead of making it harder.

POUR

We also discussed accessibility through POUR: perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust. In simple terms, users need to be able to notice the information, navigate the interface, understand what is happening, and rely on the system to work with different technologies.

Our main takeaway was that front-end accessibility is not just about making a website easier to use. In a library context, it is about whether people can access knowledge at all. If the interface creates barriers, then the library's mission of equal access is weakened. Good accessibility work helps users stay oriented, feel confident, and continue searching.

Speaker

Anna Leary is the Library Systems and Digital Services Coordinator at the Royal College of Surgeons in Ireland. Her work focuses on digital collections, library systems, digitisation, curation, and making collections easier to access and use. She has a background in library science, information management, and museum and archive collections management.

What our group appreciated about her talk was that she presented web development as part of library service, not just as a technical job. The front end of a website is often the place where users first interact with a library, so it has to work for people with different needs, devices, backgrounds, and levels of confidence.
